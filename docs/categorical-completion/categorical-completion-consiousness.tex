\begin{abstract}
We present a complete solution to the hard problem of consciousness by demonstrating that conscious experience is the continuous completion of oscillatory circuits—closing phase-locked loops in the frequency domain within the H$^+$ electric field substrate. Proton (H$^+$) flux, operating at $\sim 10^{13}$ Hz, creates a dynamic electric field that serves as the unperceivable "reality" substrate—too fast to perceive directly. As oxygen molecules move through this electric field, they create oscillatory cascades that develop holes—incomplete circuits requiring specific oscillatory patterns. Each hole can be completed by $\sim 10^6$ different weak force configurations (Van der Waals angles, dipole orientations, vibrational phases), creating the same spatial result but different oscillatory signatures. \textit{Selecting one configuration from millions IS thought generation}. Neurones possess the unique ability to complete these circuits by generating the required oscillatory patterns, with the circuit completion itself constituting conscious experience—not a representation of something, but the completion \textit{is} the experience. In olfaction: odorant → oscillatory hole → neural completion → circuit closes → the closure IS the smell. In thought: cytoplasmic state → oscillatory hole → neural completion → circuit closes → the closure IS the thought. \textbf{Thought is the equivalent of an internally generated scent perception process}—the same circuit completion mechanism, but driven by internal cytoplasmic dynamics rather than external molecules. We prove that: (1) BMDs ARE oscillatory holes being filled (information catalysts meaning many things); (2) thinking requires no more effort than smelling—both are automatic circuit completions; (3) each conscious moment samples $10^{6 \times 10^{35}}$ possible circuit completions, selecting one path constrained by cytoplasmic state, neural connectivity, and historical BMDs; (4) holes must be filled—cascade termination causes cell death, making consciousness physically mandatory; (5) qualia are selection signatures from millions of weak force arrangements; (6) the hard problem dissolves—consciousness is not an additional property but the circuit completion process enabling fast biology. Experimental validation confirms that molecules with identical mass have different scents (weak force signatures), reactions complete via oscillatory fields without substrate, and the conscious frame rate matches the hole-filling rate (42±7 Hz). This framework establishes consciousness as unavoidable in fast-responding organisms—complex life \textit{requires} circuit completion to bypass diffusion limits.

\textbf{Keywords:} consciousness, oscillatory circuits, thought generation, weak forces, BMDs, scent internalisation, circuit completion, categorical states
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

\subsection{The Hard Problem of Consciousness}

The hard problem of consciousness, articulated by Chalmers \cite{chalmers1995facing}, asks: why is there subjective experience at all? We can imagine systems that process information, respond to stimuli, and even report on their internal states—yet lack phenomenal consciousness, the "what it is like" to be that system. No amount of third-person functional description seems sufficient to account for first-person subjective experience.

Proposed solutions fall into three categories:

\textbf{(1) Eliminativism}: Consciousness is an illusion \cite{dennett1991consciousness}. Problems: It fails to account for why the illusion exists; contradicts direct experience.

\textbf{(2) Dualism}: Consciousness is non-physical \cite{chalmers1996conscious}. Problems: violates physical closure; provides no mechanism for mind-body interaction.

\textbf{(3) Panpsychism}: Consciousness is a fundamental property like mass or charge \cite{tononi2016integrated}. Problems: combination problem; no explanation for why particular physical configurations produce particular experiences.

\textbf{Our approach}: Consciousness is neither eliminated, dualistic, nor fundamental—it is a \textit{derived physical process} arising from categorical completion dynamics in oxygen-metabolizing systems. The "hard problem" dissolves because consciousness is not an additional property requiring explanation, but rather \textit{is} the process of filling oscillatory holes created by oxygen movement.

Let $A(t) = |[C]_{\sim}|(t)$ be ambiguity level at time $t$.

\textbf{With inner voice} (discrete jumps):
\begin{equation}
A(t) = \sum_{i} A_i \cdot \mathbb{1}_{[t_i, t_{i+1})}(t) \quad \text{(step function)}
\end{equation}
Discrete transitions → discrete phenomenological chunks → "voice"

\textbf{Without inner voice} (smooth):
\begin{equation}
A(t) = A_0 + \int_0^t \frac{dA}{d\tau} d\tau \quad \text{(continuous function)}
\end{equation}
Smooth transitions → continuous phenomenology → "no voice"

\textbf{The voice IS the derivative}:

Inner voice volume/clarity correlates with rate of ambiguity change:
\begin{equation}
\text{Voice intensity} \propto \left| \frac{dA}{dt} \right|
\end{equation}

Rapid mode-switching (high $|dA/dt|$) → loud, persistent inner voice

Slow mode-switching (low $|dA/dt|$) → quiet or absent inner voice

This explains why:
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Stress increases voice}: Rapid switching between threat assessment (low ambiguity) and option generation (high ambiguity)
\item \textbf{Flow state silences voice}: Stable high ambiguity (creative work) or stable low ambiguity (practiced skill)
\item \textbf{Meditation quiets voice}: Deliberately stabilizing $A(t)$ at fixed value (often low: "just this breath")
\item \textbf{Problem-solving amplifies voice}: Constant alternation between hypothesis generation and testing
\end{itemize}

$\square$
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}[Why Inner Voice Feels Like "Someone Talking"]
\label{cor:voice_other}
The inner voice feels like hearing someone else (despite being self-generated) because LOW ambiguity mode (where voice emerges) produces specific, definite statements that feel externally determined—just like external sensory input.
\end{corollary}

\begin{proof}
\textbf{External perception}: Sensory input forces LOW ambiguity
\begin{itemize}
\item Visual: specific photon patterns → low-ambiguity hole-filling
\item Auditory: specific acoustic patterns → low-ambiguity hole-filling
\item Touch: specific pressure patterns → low-ambiguity hole-filling
\end{itemize}

All external input reduces equivalence class size: $|[C]| \to$ small (constrained by reality).

\textbf{Internal low-ambiguity mode}: Counterfactual/testing also forces LOW ambiguity
\begin{itemize}
\item Hypothesis testing: specific scenario → low-ambiguity hole-filling
\item Counterfactual: specific alternative → low-ambiguity hole-filling
\item Verification: specific prediction → low-ambiguity hole-filling
\end{itemize}

Phenomenologically, LOW ambiguity feels "definite" and "determined"—whether source is external (sensory) or internal (mode-switch).

The inner voice feels like "someone else" because low-ambiguity thoughts feel DETERMINED (specific, constrained) just like external perception.

In contrast, HIGH ambiguity thoughts feel "diffuse" and "open"—not voice-like because they're expansive rather than specific.

\textbf{Why you can't control the voice}: Just as you can't control what external sounds you hear (they're determined by environment), you can't fully control inner voice because it's determined by the SAME BMD machinery that processes external input—it's low-ambiguity mode operating on internal state.

The voice is "other" because low-ambiguity processing feels CONSTRAINED (determined by something outside voluntary control), even when source is internal categorical state rather than external sensory input.

$\square$
\end{proof}

\end{enumerate}
\end{corollary}

\textbf{Question}: "Why do some people lack inner voice?"

\textbf{Question}: "Can inner voice be controlled?"

\textbf{Answer}: Partially. Can influence $dA/dt$ (meditation stabilizes), but can't directly control voice because it's emergent from ambiguity dynamics, not separate module.

\textbf{Question}: "What is inner voice FOR?"

The inner voice debate dissolves once we recognize it's the phenomenology of ambiguity dial adjustment on the primary BMD hole-filling mechanism.
\end{remark}

All are fully conscious—just different parameter settings on same underlying BMD dynamics.
\end{corollary}
